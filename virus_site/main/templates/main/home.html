OVERVIEW

Orthohantaviruses are enveloped, negative-sense RNA viruses belonging to the Hantaviridae family, primarily maintained in rodent reservoirs that shed the virus through urine, feces, and saliva. Their genome consists of three segments—Small (S), Medium (M), and Large (L)—which encode the Nucleocapsid (N) protein, Glycoproteins Gn and Gc, and the RNA-Dependent RNA Polymerase (RdRp), respectively.

These viruses cause two major human diseases: Hemorrhagic Fever with Renal Syndrome (HFRS), mainly in Europe and Asia, and Hantavirus Cardiopulmonary Syndrome (HCPS) in America. Transmission to humans typically occurs through inhalation of aerosolized rodent excreta.

Orthohantavirus infection targets endothelial cells, leading to increased vascular permeability and immune-mediated tissue damage. Clinical outcomes vary by virus species, with HFRS fatality rates ranging from less than 1% to 15%, and HCPS mortality reaching up to 60%.

Diagnosis relies on molecular and serological methods, while treatment remains supportive as no specific antivirals or vaccines are currently approved. Ongoing research focuses on understanding viral protein functions, host interactions, and genomic diversity to aid in the development of effective therapeutics and preventive measures. This genomic archive compiles comprehensive data on all three viral segments to support such efforts.

MISSION & PURPOSE

Our goal is to centralize, curate, and simplify access to OrthoHantavirus genomic data for use in research, surveillance, diagnostics, and drug development.

TAXONOMIC OVERVIEW

OrthoHantavirus comprises a diverse array of rodent-, shrew-, mole-, and bat-associated viruses. Based on ICTV classification and recent genomic data, species are broadly divided into classified and provisionally unclassified groups.

DID YOU KNOW?

  1. OrthoHantaviruses have co-evolved with specific rodent hosts for millions of years, leading to strict virus-host pairings.
  2. The Andes virus is the only known hantavirus that can transmit person-to-person, with outbreaks reported in Chile and Argentina.
  3. Infected rodents shed the virus in saliva, urine, and feces — but remain asymptomatic carriers for life.
  4. Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome (HPS) was first recognized in 1993 in the U.S. Four Corners region, with deer mice identified as the source.
  5. Although no global vaccine exists, China uses an inactivated vaccine for Hantaan and Seoul viruses, and new DNA and VLP vaccines are in development.
  6. Outbreaks often follow "mast years" when oak or beech trees produce excess seeds, causing rodent populations (like voles) to spike.
